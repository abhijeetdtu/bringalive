Convert the text into a compact bullet summary without losing any explicit information, and place a full stop after every sentence so the output works cleanly with TTS.

Core rule:
- Compression is allowed.
- Omission is not allowed.

Goal:
- Make the text shorter and easier to scan.
- Preserve every explicit fact needed to reconstruct the original meaning.
- Ensure punctuation supports natural speech rhythm for text-to-speech.

Write as:
- compact bullets.
- information-dense bullets.
- one bullet per atomic fact or tightly linked fact group.
- sub-bullets when needed to preserve detail, causality, sequence, or explanation.

Preserve explicitly:
- full names of people, places, organizations, institutions, works, objects, vehicles, and locations.
- dates, years, times, eras, and numbers.
- who did what.
- what exactly happened.
- what physically happened, if stated.
- causes, mechanisms, and means.
- sequence and chronology.
- consequences and outcomes.
- quoted statements and key phrases.
- qualifiers, conditions, limitations, contrast, and uncertainty.
- relationships between people, places, events, and ideas.

Critical rules:
1. Do not omit any explicit fact.
2. Do not replace a specific event with a more general label.
3. Do not replace a stated mechanism with a vague category.
4. Do not merge separate facts if that makes any part less specific.
5. Do not flatten scenes, events, or claims into note-like tags such as "incident," "issue," "development," or "critique" unless the source itself uses them that way.
6. Do not invent facts, links, or interpretation.
7. Do not generalize beyond the source.
8. Keep concrete nouns and verbs from the source where possible.
9. If one sentence contains multiple explicit details, split them into separate bullets or sub-bullets rather than dropping any.
10. Prefer one extra bullet over one over-compressed bullet.

Rules for names, abbreviations, and shorthand:
11. On first mention, always use the full name exactly as given in the source.
12. Never use a short form, acronym, or initialism on first mention unless the full form appears in the same bullet.
13. After first mention, shorthand may be used only if it is unambiguous.
14. Do not use pronouns or shortened references if the reader may not know what they refer to.
15. Each bullet should remain understandable on its own.

Rules for event fidelity:
16. If the source states what physically happened, preserve that exact action.
17. Do not replace "the coach toppled over" with "a coach accident" or any broader phrase.
18. If the source gives both result and mechanism, preserve both.
19. Keep causality explicit when the source makes it explicit.
20. Keep chronology explicit when the order matters.

Rules for punctuation and TTS readability:
21. Use correct punctuation throughout.
22. Put a full stop after every sentence.
23. End every bullet with a full stop.
24. End every sub-bullet with a full stop.
25. If a bullet contains more than one sentence, each sentence must end with a full stop.
26. Write each bullet as natural spoken language, not broken telegraphic fragments.
27. Use commas, colons, and semicolons only when they improve clarity and TTS pauses.
28. Avoid excessive dashes, slashes, stacked parentheses, or cryptic abbreviations that sound unnatural in speech.
29. If a bullet contains a cause, contrast, apposition, or sequence, punctuate it so TTS pauses correctly.
30. Prefer full words over symbolic shorthand when symbols may sound awkward in speech.
31. Expand ambiguous shorthand if it would hurt spoken clarity.
32. Keep quoted material punctuated correctly.
33. If a bullet has sub-bullets, make each sub-bullet independently readable in speech.
34. Do not produce notes that sound incomplete when read aloud.

Style:
- crisp.
- compact.
- readable.
- faithful.
- TTS-friendly.
- shorthand is allowed only when it preserves full meaning and still sounds natural when spoken.

Method:
- Decompose each sentence into all explicit facts.
- Preserve each fact in a bullet or sub-bullet.
- Rephrase only enough to add punctuation, clarity, and natural TTS flow.
- Re-read the summary mentally as spoken audio before finalizing.

Final check before answering:
- Did I replace any specific event with a broader category?
- Did I drop any mechanism, object, or causal step?
- Did I introduce any unexplained short form?
- Did I omit any name, place, date, number, quote, qualifier, or consequence?
- Would a new reader understand each bullet without seeing the source?
- Would each bullet sound natural if read by a TTS engine?
- Does every sentence end with a full stop?

If any answer is yes, fix it before output.

Output only the bullet summary.
